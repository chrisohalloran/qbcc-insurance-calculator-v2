QBCC Insurance: New Construction vs Renovation Premiums

The QBCC home warranty insurance scheme uses two separate premium tables — one for new construction and one for renovations. The rates are different, and not in the way most people expect. Renovation premiums are higher than new construction for most residential projects, but for large projects over roughly $607,000, that relationship flips.

This guide shows you exactly which table applies, what you'll pay at every common project value, and why the rates differ.

Which premium table applies to your project?

The QBCC uses your contractor's scope of work — not the project value — to determine which table applies.

Your project Premium table
Building a new residence (house, duplex, townhouse, villa) New construction
Alterations, additions, or extensions to an existing home Renovation
Repairs to an existing residence Renovation
Construction of a swimming pool Renovation
Construction of a related roofed building (shed, carport, garage) Renovation
Kitchen, bathroom, or structural renovation Renovation

The renovation table covers a much broader range of work. Any licensed contractor doing residential building work valued above $3,300 (materials + labour + GST) must take out insurance using the appropriate table. Source: when is QBCC insurance required.

The counterintuitive crossover point

Most builders and homeowners assume renovation insurance always costs more than new construction — for the same dollar value of work. That assumption is wrong for large projects.

At approximately $607,000, the two premium tables produce almost identical figures ($6,768.65 for new construction versus $6,769.85 for renovation). Above that value, new construction premiums grow faster and become the more expensive option.

For a $800,000 project, a builder constructing a new home pays $9,702.35 in insurance — versus $7,392.55 for a renovation contractor doing the same dollar value of work. That's a $2,309.80 difference in favour of the renovation table.

This isn't a pricing error. It reflects deliberate policy design: the QBCC charges a premium that reflects the risk profile of each project type. Structural defect claims tend to be more complex on new builds at high values, which drives the rate difference.

Why renovation rates start higher for most projects

The rate structure explanation requires looking at how each table charges per additional $1,000 of insurable value.

Renovation rate structure

The renovation table charges roughly $7.15 per additional $1,000 at low project values (under $50k), stepping up to approximately $15.75 per $1,000 at values from $100k to $250k. This reflects the complexity of integrating new work into an existing structure — plumbing, structural tie-ins, and waterproofing in existing homes have consistently high defect rates.

New construction rate structure

The new construction table is structured very differently. For projects up to $250,000, the rate is approximately $4.65 per additional $1,000. At $250,001, the rate jumps dramatically to around $15.25 per $1,000 — nearly the same as the renovation rate — and stays there until $800,000.

This jump at $250,000 is one of the most important facts in the QBCC premium tables. A builder quoting a $249,000 new home pays a dramatically lower premium than one quoting $251,000. See the full rate-band breakdown in our QBCC premium rates guide.

Why the tables converge and cross

Above approximately $600,000, the renovation table's per-$1,000 increment drops to roughly $3.20 per $1,000 — well below the new construction rate of $15.25 per $1,000 that is still charging in that band. The renovation table is designed to flatten out at high project values; the new construction table maintains its steep mid-range rate all the way to $800,000.

The result: renovation starts more expensive, the gap narrows through $400–$600k, and new construction overtakes renovation at the $607k crossover.

How to calculate your insurable value

Regardless of which table applies, the insurable value is calculated the same way.

Include:

  • All materials (including materials supplied by the homeowner)
  • All labour costs
  • GST
  • Associated work in the contract (driveways, landscaping, fencing, air conditioning, hot water systems)

Exclude:

  • The QBCC insurance premium itself
  • The QLeave levy (for projects over $150,000 excluding GST — see our QLeave levy guide)

The QBCC tables round to the nearest $1,000. Any amount above a $1,000 threshold requires payment at the next level. For example, an insurable value of $301,500 uses the $302,000 premium row.

Frequently asked questions

My builder is adding a second storey. Which table applies?

A second storey addition to an existing home is an alteration or extension. It uses the renovation table, not the new construction table. Only the construction of an entirely new residence uses the new construction table.

Does a knockdown-and-rebuild use the new construction table?

Yes. If the existing structure is demolished and a new residence is built on the land, the new construction premium table applies. The QBCC treats this as building a new home.

Why does my quote show a renovation premium that's much higher than I expected?

The renovation table charges approximately $15.75 per additional $1,000 of value in the $100k–$600k range — significantly more than the new construction rate below $250k. A $200,000 renovation project attracts an 85% higher premium than a new construction at the same value. This is the most common source of bill shock for renovation clients.

Are the premium tables the same across all of Queensland?

Yes. The QBCC premium tables are set by state government legislation and apply uniformly across Queensland regardless of location, builder, or project complexity. Both tables have been effective since 1 July 2020.
