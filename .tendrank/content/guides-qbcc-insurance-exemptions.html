QBCC Insurance Exemptions: When It's Not Required

Most residential building work in Queensland over $3,300 requires QBCC home warranty insurance — but several categories of work are legally exempt. If your project is an owner-builder job, on commercial premises, or falls below the value threshold, no insurance premium is payable. This guide lists every exemption, with the exact legislative basis for each, sourced from the QBCC A to Z Guide of Insurable Building Work (July 2024).

The $3,300 Value Threshold

Home warranty insurance is only required for residential construction work with a primary insurable component valued at more than $3,300 (including materials, labour, and GST). A contract below this threshold is not residential construction work for insurance purposes — no premium is payable on that contract alone.

Two important clarifications:

  • Bundled contracts: If work below $3,300 is included in a larger contract that does include primary insurable work, the entire contract value (including the lower-value items) is subject to the premium. For example, a $2,000 fence combined with a $50,000 renovation means the full $52,000 is the insurable value.
  • Amended contracts: If a project's scope is reduced and the total value falls below $3,300, no insurance is required. Source: QBCC homeowner guide (reviewed August 2024).

Owner-Builder Projects

This is the most common exemption question for homeowners who want to manage their own build.

Owner-builders with a valid QBCC permit are not covered by — and do not pay into — the Queensland Home Warranty Scheme. According to the QBCC A-Z Guide (July 2024), "building work carried out by a building contractor for a person who is the holder of an owner builder permit covering the work is not insurable" (s 67WB(1)(b) of the QBCC Act 1991).

This means:

  • The homeowner (owner-builder) cannot claim under the scheme if something goes wrong.
  • Subcontractors working for the owner-builder do not pay premiums for that work — provided the owner-builder holds the correct permit covering it.
  • Owner-builders are also exempt from the QLeave levy on work done under their permit — but only if they present their QBCC owner-builder permit number to QLeave directly. See our QLeave levy guide for how this works.

Critical trap for subcontractors: If you subcontract to an owner-builder and they do not have the correct permit for the work you're doing, you may be legally obliged to take out home warranty insurance yourself. Always verify the permit covers the specific scope of work. Source: QBCC, "Who pays home warranty and when."

Owner-builder permit eligibility

A QBCC owner-builder permit is required for projects over $11,000 on residential property ($27,500 for farm buildings). You can only hold one permit every 6 years. Most applicants must complete an approved owner-builder course before applying. Source: QBCC, "About Owner-building."

Commercial and Non-Residential Work

Building work on commercial or industrial premises is excluded entirely from the Queensland Home Warranty Scheme (s 67WB(1)(a)(viii)).

This covers:

  • Offices, shops, warehouses, factories
  • Industrial sites
  • Building work that is part of a site classified as commercial or industrial, even if some residential elements exist

Other non-residential exclusions under s 67WB include:

  • Registered retirement villages (s 67WB(1)(a)(v))
  • Schools, TAFEs, universities, and educational institutions (s 67WB(1)(a)(vi))
  • Hospitals, nursing homes, healthcare buildings, and doctor's surgeries (s 67WB(1)(a)(iii))
  • Hotels, motels, boarding houses, hostels, and lodging houses (s 67WB(1)(a)(i))
  • Prisons and watch houses (s 67WB(1)(a)(ii))
  • Orphanages (s 67WB(1)(a)(iv))
  • Group accommodation for persons with a physical or mental disability (s 67WB(1)(a)(vii))

Note on Airbnb units: Where one unit in a multi-dwelling building is used for short-term holiday rental (e.g. Airbnb), that specific unit is classified as "holiday accommodation" and is excluded. Other units in the same building used for permanent residential purposes may still be insurable. Source: QBCC A-Z Guide, "Holiday Accommodation — Multiple dwelling – units."

Multi-Storey Buildings (More Than 3 Storeys)

Residential buildings with more than 3 storeys are excluded from the Queensland Home Warranty Scheme. This includes work on individual units within such buildings. The exclusion applies to both the main building and any duplexes over 3 storeys (s 67WE(1)(c); Regulation 2018, s 26(a)).

Car park levels: When counting storeys, one storey may be excluded from the count if more than 50% of its floor area is used for parking. A building with 4 storeys where the ground floor is entirely car parking may be counted as 3 storeys and could still be insurable.

Buildings of 3 storeys or fewer with 2 or more separate residential units are considered residences and are insurable where the work exceeds $3,300.

Farm Buildings

Work on a farm building is not "building work" under Queensland law and is therefore never insurable — not even as associated insurable work bundled with a residence contract (Regulation 2018, s 1, Schedule 1).

A farm building must meet all four criteria:

  1. On land used wholly or primarily for agricultural or pastoral purposes
  2. Used (or intended to be used) for agricultural or pastoral purposes
  3. Not a residence
  4. Completed value of not more than $27,500

Examples: feed storage sheds, equipment shelters, fertilizer storage on a working farm.

A residence on farm land — even a farmer's home — is insurable. The exemption is for the farm structures, not the dwelling itself.

Licensed Contractors Building Their Own Home

This exemption surprises many people. A licensed building contractor who builds or renovates their own property does not need to pay QBCC insurance premiums if they have no intention to sell within 6 years and 6 months of commencing construction.

Requirements:

  • The owner of the land must be the licensed contractor personally doing the work (not a company they own)
  • They must lodge a statutory declaration with QBCC stating the property is not intended for sale
  • If the property is subsequently listed for sale within 6 years and 6 months, the premium becomes payable at that time

The entity trap: If a licensed contractor holds land personally but the building work is performed under their company's licence (rather than their individual licence), this exemption does not apply — a contract exists between the individual and the company, and a premium is payable. Source: QBCC A-Z Guide, "Work carried out on own home vs speculative work for licenced contractors."

Work That Is Not Standalone Insurable (But Can Be Covered)

Some types of work are not "primary insurable work" on their own — you do not pay a premium if the contract covers only these items. However, if they are included in a larger contract that does include primary insurable work, the whole contract value (including these items) is subject to the premium.

Work in this category includes (Regulation 2018, ss 28, 67WC(2)):

Work type Standalone: premium payable? In a bundled contract?
Fencing (including pool fencing) No Yes — value included
Driveways No Yes — value included
Solar panels No Yes — value included
Air conditioning (ducted/split) No Yes — value included
Hot water systems (gas/solar) No Yes — value included
Security doors/screens No Yes — value included
Freestanding decks (not attached to residence) No Yes — value included
Shade sails (standalone) No Yes — value included
Asbestos removal No Yes — value included

This distinction matters: a licensed electrician installing $8,000 of solar panels alone does not pay a QBCC premium. But the same solar panels in a $400,000 new home contract increase the insurable value used to calculate the premium. Cost-Plus Contracts

Home warranty insurance technically applies to cost-plus contracts, but the coverage is severely limited. Because the final price is unknown, QBCC cannot settle a non-completion claim — it cannot quantify what you agreed to pay to finish the work. Only structural defect and non-structural defect claims remain available.

The QBCC strongly recommends avoiding cost-plus contracts for this reason. If you are a homeowner, insist on a fixed-price contract to ensure full protection under the scheme. Learn more about when home warranty insurance is required in our complete guide to when QBCC insurance is required.

Common Questions

Is QBCC insurance required for a bathroom renovation?

Yes, if the contract value exceeds $3,300 (including materials, labour, and GST). Bathroom renovation is classified as primary insurable work under s 67WC(1)(b) of the QBCC Act 1991. The licensed contractor must take out cover before work begins.

Do owner-builders pay QLeave levy?

No — owner-builders are exempt from the QLeave levy on work carried out under their permit, provided they present their owner-builder permit number to QLeave directly. The exemption must be applied for; it is not automatic. See our QLeave levy guide for details.

Is a granny flat (secondary dwelling) insurable?

Yes. A granny flat on the same residential block as the main dwelling is a "related roofed building" if it is a fixed structure used for a purpose related to the residence. Work on it over $3,300 is primary insurable work. Source: QBCC A-Z Guide, Glossary — "Related roofed building."

Does insurance apply to a shed or carport?

Yes, if the shed or carport is a "related roofed building" — fixed structure, on the site of a residence, used for a residential purpose, with a watertight roof. A farm shed used for agricultural purposes (not residential) is not insurable. Source: QBCC A-Z Guide, "Shed, carport, garage."

Is a swimming pool insurable?

The initial construction or installation of a fixed swimming pool (inground or above-ground, but fixed to the ground) is primary insurable work over $3,300 (s 67WC(1)(d)). The renovation or repair of an existing pool is not primary insurable work on a standalone contract — though it becomes associated insurable work if bundled with other primary work.

What happens if a builder doesn't take out insurance?

Failure to take out home warranty insurance is a serious offence in Queensland. The builder faces significant penalties and the homeowner may be left without protection. The QBCC requires cover to be in place before work begins. For a full breakdown of cover timing and claim limits, see our QBCC insurance FAQ.
